What is TEEL?

TEEL is a way to build a clear paragraph. It helps you keep your ideas in order so your reader can understand your thinking. TEEL stands for:

T – Topic Sentence

The main idea of your paragraph. It tells the reader what your paragraph will be about.

Common Words: One important idea is..., The main point is..., This paragraph will explain...

Example: "One important idea is that teamwork helps a group solve problems."

E – Evidence

Proof from the text. This is a quote or a clear description of a moment in the story.

Common Words: For example..., The text states..., When the author says...

Example: "For example, the class survey showed that students work better when instructions are clear."

E – Explanation

Your thinking. You explain how your evidence supports your topic sentence.

Common Words: This shows that..., This means..., This helps the reader see...

Example: "This shows that clear instructions can help everyone understand the task."

L – Link

The closing idea. You link back to the topic sentence or to a bigger idea in the story.

Common Words: Overall..., As a result..., This proves that...

Example: "Overall, this proves that clear thinking is an important part of good writing."

TEEL PARAGRAPH BUILDER

Great work. You have trained with TEEL. Now it is time to write your own paragraph. In this section you will write ONE TEEL paragraph to explain a topic or idea of your choice.

Step 1: Your Topic

First, choose the topic or big idea that your paragraph is about. Write it in the box below.

Examples of topics you might choose:

  • School uniforms
  • Longer recess
  • Healthy lunches
  • Reading for pleasure
  • Using devices at school

Step 2: T - Topic Sentence

T is for TOPIC SENTENCE

What it does: Says your main idea in one clear sentence. It must name your topic and show the reader what your paragraph will explain.

Ask yourself:

  • What topic did I choose?
  • How will I support my idea with evidence?

Tip: In one sentence, say your topic and tell the reader what the paragraph will be about.

Example: "My paragraph explains why clear routines help students learn, because each example shows how structure makes school life easier."

Step 3: E - Evidence

E is for EVIDENCE

What it does: Gives a strong quote or a clear description. This proves that your topic is important.

Ask yourself:

  • Which moment or detail matches my topic?
  • Can I include a clear detail or quote to show where it comes from?

Tip: Choose one detail that connects well to your topic.

Example: "For example, the class survey showed that students worked better when they had a clear model, which supports the idea that structure helps learning."

Step 4: E - Explanation

E is for EXPLANATION

What it does: Explains how your evidence supports your topic. This is your thinking in your own words.

Ask yourself:

  • How does my quote or detail show this idea?
  • How does my explanation connect the evidence to the topic sentence?

Tip: Try to explain the link between your topic sentence and your evidence in your own words.

Example: "This shows that students are more confident when they have a clear model, because it gives them a guide for what to do next."

Step 5: L - Link

L is for LINK

What it does: Finishes your paragraph. It links back to your topic and shows why your idea is important.

Ask yourself:

  • What do all my sentences show when we look at them together?
  • Why is this topic important to the reader?

Tip: Zoom out. Think about the whole paragraph, not just one detail.

Example: "Overall, these sentences show that clear routines help students learn, proving that structure is an important idea in this paragraph."
